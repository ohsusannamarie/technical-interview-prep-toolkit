# Questions to Ask Interviewers

Good candidate questions do two things:

1. They help you evaluate whether the role is actually a fit.
2. They show the interviewer how you think.

You do not need to ask questions that sound impressive. Ask questions that reveal the truth.

## Quick picks

If you only remember a few, use these:

- What would success look like in the first 3-6 months?
- What are the biggest technical challenges facing the team right now?
- What would make someone thrive in this role?
- What would make someone struggle?
- How does the team make technical decisions?
- What is one thing you wish candidates understood before joining this team?

## Questions for recruiters

Use these to clarify the process and avoid preparing for the wrong thing.

- What interview stages should I expect?
- Will there be live coding, system design, a project deep dive, behavioral questions, or a take-home?
- What skills are most important for this role?
- Is there a preferred programming language or technical environment?
- How is the team structured?
- What is the hiring team hoping this person solves?
- Is this a new role or a backfill?
- What is the expected level or scope?
- What is the compensation range for the role?
- What is the remote, hybrid, relocation, or travel expectation?

## Questions for hiring managers

Use these to understand the team, expectations, and actual work.

- What would success look like in the first 3 months?
- What would success look like in the first 6-12 months?
- What are the most important problems this person will own?
- What is currently harder than it should be for the team?
- How do you define strong performance for this role?
- What kind of person tends to thrive on this team?
- What kind of person tends to struggle?
- How do you prefer to give feedback?
- How are priorities set?
- What is the balance between planned work and interrupt-driven work?

## Questions for technical interviewers

Use these to understand engineering culture and technical reality.

- How does the team make architecture decisions?
- What technical tradeoffs has the team made recently?
- How does code review work here?
- How does the team handle technical debt?
- How are incidents handled?
- What does observability look like for the systems this team owns?
- How do engineers work with product, design, data, hardware, or other teams?
- What is one technical problem here that is harder than it looks from the outside?
- What parts of the system are you most excited to improve?
- What engineering habits does this team value most?

## Questions for senior, staff, or principal roles

Use these to clarify scope, influence, and expectations.

- What level of ambiguity should this person be comfortable owning?
- Where does the team need stronger technical direction?
- How much influence does this role have across teams?
- What decisions would this person be expected to drive?
- How do staff+ engineers create impact here?
- What are the current alignment challenges across teams?
- How are technical strategy and roadmap decisions made?
- What would make someone successful beyond their own direct output?

## Questions for engineering manager roles

- What is the current team structure?
- What are the biggest people leadership challenges on the team?
- How are goals set and measured?
- How does the company balance delivery, quality, and team health?
- What support exists for manager development?
- What is the expectation around technical depth versus people leadership?
- How is performance management handled?
- What does the team need most from its next manager?

## Questions for hardware / semiconductor roles

- What part of the design or product lifecycle would this role support?
- What tools, flows, or methodologies are most important here?
- What are the biggest schedule, quality, or performance pressures on the team?
- How do design, verification, physical design, firmware, validation, and systems teams collaborate?
- What are the most common bottlenecks in the current workflow?
- How are tradeoffs made around power, performance, area, cost, and schedule?
- What does strong debugging look like on this team?

## Questions about team culture without asking “what is the culture like?”

The phrase “what is the culture like?” often gets a brochure answer.

Ask these instead:

- How does the team handle disagreement?
- What happens when priorities change suddenly?
- How are mistakes handled?
- How does the team share knowledge?
- What meetings are actually useful here?
- How does the team protect focus time?
- What behaviors are rewarded here?
- What is something the team is actively trying to improve?

## Questions to be careful with

These are not bad questions, but ask them thoughtfully.

| Question | Why to be careful |
|---|---|
| Why is this role open? | Useful, but tone matters. Ask with curiosity, not suspicion. |
| What is work-life balance like? | Important, but often gets vague answers. Ask about norms and expectations instead. |
| How much technical debt is there? | Every team has some. Ask how it is prioritized. |
| What is the culture like? | Too broad. Ask behavior-based questions instead. |

## A strong closing question

```text
Based on what we discussed today, is there anything about my background that you would want me to clarify or expand on?
```

This gives you a chance to address concerns before the interview ends.

## Final reminder

You are interviewing them too.

That does not mean grilling people like a courtroom drama. It means gathering enough signal to make a good decision about your own future.

# Technical Interview Types

Not all technical interviews are testing the same thing.

A coding screen, a system design conversation, a hiring manager screen, and a behavioral interview are different games. The fastest way to prepare better is to understand which game you are playing.

## Interview type overview

| Interview type | What it usually tests | How to prepare |
|---|---|---|
| Recruiter screen | Motivation, role fit, logistics, communication | Know your story, priorities, constraints, and questions |
| Hiring manager screen | Role fit, technical depth, collaboration style | Prepare project examples and role-specific context |
| Live coding | Problem-solving, data structures, communication, debugging | Practice patterns, edge cases, and explaining your thinking |
| Take-home assignment | Practical execution, code quality, product thinking, follow-through | Clarify scope, write readable code, document tradeoffs |
| System design | Architecture, scale, tradeoffs, reliability, constraints | Practice structured answers and tradeoff narration |
| Project deep dive | Technical depth, ownership, impact, judgment | Prepare 1-2 detailed project stories |
| Behavioral interview | Collaboration, conflict, ownership, ambiguity, learning | Prepare adaptable stories with context and outcomes |
| Panel interview | Consistency, communication, cross-functional fit | Keep answers clear, concise, and audience-aware |

## Recruiter screen

Recruiter screens are not throwaway calls. They often shape how you are represented to the hiring team.

Be ready to explain:

- What you are looking for next
- Why this role or company interests you
- What parts of your experience are most relevant
- Location, remote, hybrid, relocation, or travel needs
- Work authorization status, if relevant
- Compensation expectations or constraints
- Interview availability and timing

Strong signal:

> You can explain your background clearly and help the recruiter understand where you fit.

## Hiring manager screen

This is usually about role fit, technical judgment, and how you would work with the team.

Prepare to discuss:

- Relevant projects
- Technical decisions you made
- How you collaborate
- What kind of problems you like solving
- What you need from a manager or team to do your best work
- What you are still learning

Strong signal:

> You can connect your experience to the actual needs of the team.

## Live coding interview

Live coding is usually testing more than whether you can reach the answer.

Interviewers may be watching for:

- Problem breakdown
- Clarifying questions
- Data structure choice
- Edge case awareness
- Code readability
- Debugging approach
- Time management
- Communication under pressure

Good prep:

- Practice common patterns
- Talk out loud while solving
- Explain tradeoffs before coding
- Test with simple and edge cases
- Practice recovering when stuck

Strong signal:

> You can solve problems collaboratively and make your thinking visible.

## Take-home assignment

Take-homes often test practical engineering habits.

Clarify:

- Expected time investment
- Scope and deliverables
- Language or framework constraints
- Whether tests are expected
- What the review criteria are

Good submission habits:

- Keep the solution scoped
- Include clear setup instructions
- Explain assumptions
- Document tradeoffs
- Add tests where useful
- Avoid overengineering unless the prompt asks for it

Strong signal:

> You can build something understandable, useful, and maintainable without turning it into a six-season prestige drama.

## System design interview

System design interviews usually test how you reason through ambiguity.

Common evaluation areas:

- Requirements gathering
- Constraints
- APIs
- Data models
- Storage choices
- Caching
- Queues and messaging
- Scale
- Reliability
- Observability
- Security and privacy
- Tradeoffs

Use the [System Design Answer Framework](system-design-answer-framework.md) for a repeatable structure.

Strong signal:

> You can ask good questions, make reasonable assumptions, and explain tradeoffs clearly.

## Project deep dive

Project deep dives test whether you can explain work you have actually done.

Prepare 1-2 projects where you can explain:

- The problem
- Why it mattered
- Your specific role
- Technical decisions
- Constraints
- Tradeoffs
- Results or impact
- What you would improve now

Use the [Engineering Story Bank](engineering-story-bank.md) to prepare.

Strong signal:

> You can show ownership, judgment, and impact - not just list technologies.

## Behavioral interview

Behavioral interviews test how you work with people, pressure, ambiguity, and mistakes.

Prepare stories about:

- Conflict or disagreement
- Failure or mistake
- Ambiguity
- Learning quickly
- Influencing without authority
- Collaboration
- Mentorship
- Ownership

Strong signal:

> You can reflect honestly, communicate clearly, and show mature judgment.

## Panel interview

Panel interviews can feel intense because multiple people may be evaluating different things at once.

Helpful tactics:

- Answer the person who asked first, then include the room
- Keep answers structured
- Ask clarifying questions when needed
- Do not ramble to fill silence
- Bring answers back to the role
- Track repeated themes across interviewer questions

Strong signal:

> You can communicate clearly to different stakeholders without losing the thread.

## If you are unsure what type of interview it is

Ask the recruiter:

```text
Could you share a little more about the interview format so I can prepare appropriately? For example, should I expect live coding, system design, a project discussion, behavioral questions, or something else?
```

A reasonable hiring process should be able to answer that.

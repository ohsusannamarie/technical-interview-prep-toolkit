# Technical Interview Prep Toolkit

Technical interview prep for real humans - not just algorithm machines.

This is a practical, candidate-friendly toolkit for preparing for technical interviews across software, data, ML/AI, systems, hardware, semiconductor, and engineering leadership roles.

Technical interviews can be overwhelming, especially when candidates are handed a pile of links with no context. This toolkit organizes prep into a clearer path: what kind of interview you are walking into, what to practice, how to communicate your thinking, and how to advocate for yourself along the way.

Built from a recruiting, sourcing, and talent intelligence perspective, this is meant to be useful, shareable, and human - not another intimidating mega-list.

## Who this is for

- **Candidates** preparing for software engineering, technical, data, ML, hardware, or systems interviews
- **Recruiters and sourcers** who want better prep materials to share with candidates
- **Hiring teams** who want to reduce ambiguity and improve candidate experience
- **Career changers and early-career engineers** looking for a practical starting point
- **Neurodivergent candidates** who may benefit from clearer structure, scripts, and self-advocacy tools

## Start here

If you only have a few days before an interview, do not try to boil the ocean. Focus on the highest-signal prep:

1. Clarify the interview format
2. Review common coding or role-specific patterns
3. Practice explaining your thinking out loud
4. Prepare 3-5 project stories
5. Research the company, product, team, or technical domain
6. Prepare thoughtful questions for the interviewer
7. Know how to recover if you get stuck

## How to use this toolkit

### I have 48 hours before an interview

Focus on:

- Confirming the interview format
- Practicing a few common problem patterns
- Speaking your thought process out loud
- Preparing project examples
- Writing down questions for the team
- Testing your virtual setup

Do not cram 200 problems. That is panic wearing a fake mustache.

### I have 2 weeks

Focus on:

- Daily coding practice by pattern
- System design fundamentals
- Mock interviews or timed practice
- Behavioral stories using STAR, CAR, or the project story bank
- Company and role-specific prep
- A stronger question list for evaluating fit

### I am a recruiter sharing this with a candidate

Send the sections that match the interview. Do not dump the entire internet into a candidate's lap and call it enablement.

Good prep reduces anxiety. Great prep reduces ambiguity.

## Core prep areas

### Coding practice

Use coding practice platforms to build pattern recognition, speed, and confidence.

Common focus areas:

- Arrays and strings
- Hash maps and sets
- Two pointers
- Sliding window
- Stacks and queues
- Trees and graphs
- Recursion
- Dynamic programming basics
- Sorting and searching
- Big O notation

### System design

System design interviews usually test how you reason through tradeoffs, scale, reliability, and communication.

Common focus areas:

- Requirements and constraints
- APIs
- Data models
- Databases
- Caching
- Load balancing
- Queues and messaging
- Availability and reliability
- Latency and throughput
- Observability
- Tradeoff communication

Start with the [System Design Answer Framework](system-design-answer-framework.md).

### Behavioral interview prep

Technical skill matters, but so does the ability to explain how you work.

Prepare stories about:

- A difficult technical problem
- A time you disagreed with a teammate
- A project you influenced without authority
- A failure or mistake
- A time you improved a system or process
- A cross-functional collaboration
- A time you had to learn something quickly

Start with the [Engineering Story Bank](engineering-story-bank.md).

### Recruiter screen prep

Recruiter screens are not throwaway calls. They often shape how you are represented to the hiring team.

Be ready to discuss:

- What you are looking for next
- Why this role interests you
- Compensation expectations or constraints
- Location, relocation, remote, or hybrid preferences
- Work authorization needs
- Interview timing and availability
- The strongest parts of your background for the role

### Technical communication

A strong answer is not just correct. It is followable.

Practice:

- Explaining assumptions
- Asking clarifying questions
- Narrating tradeoffs
- Naming constraints
- Talking through edge cases
- Summarizing your approach before diving in

## Candidate prep checklist

Before the interview:

- [ ] I understand the interview format
- [ ] I know whether I will be coding live, completing a take-home, or discussing past work
- [ ] I know which language, tools, or environment I can use
- [ ] I practiced explaining my thought process out loud
- [ ] I prepared 3-5 project stories
- [ ] I reviewed the company, product, team, or domain
- [ ] I have thoughtful questions ready
- [ ] I know who to contact if something goes wrong

See the full checklist: [candidate-checklist.md](candidate-checklist.md)

## For recruiters

When sharing prep resources with candidates, context matters.

Instead of sending a giant list of links, tell candidates:

- What type of interview to expect
- What skills will likely be assessed
- Whether the interview is language-specific
- Whether they should prioritize speed, accuracy, communication, architecture, or tradeoff thinking
- How much time they should realistically spend preparing

The goal is not to give candidates an unfair advantage. The goal is to remove mystery so the interview measures skill instead of anxiety tolerance.

## Repository philosophy

This repo is intentionally practical.

It is not trying to be the biggest technical interview prep list on GitHub. There are already enough giant lists that make people feel like they need a second lifetime and a minor in panic.

This toolkit is meant to help people answer three questions:

1. What kind of interview am I preparing for?
2. What should I focus on first?
3. How do I walk in with more clarity and less chaos?

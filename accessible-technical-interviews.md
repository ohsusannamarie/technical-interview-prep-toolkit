# Accessible Technical Interviews

Technical interviews often reward candidates who can think quickly, talk continuously, process vague instructions in real time, and perform calmly under observation.

That is not the same thing as technical ability.

This guide is for candidates who benefit from clearer structure, written context, processing time, reduced ambiguity, or accommodation support. It may be especially useful for neurodivergent candidates, candidates with anxiety, candidates with disabilities, candidates interviewing in a second language, or anyone who does better when expectations are explicit.

## First: you are allowed to ask for clarity

Asking clarifying questions is not weakness. In technical work, it is often the job.

Useful phrases:

```text
Could you clarify what you would like me to optimize for?
```

```text
Can I restate the prompt to make sure I understood it correctly?
```

```text
Would you prefer I start with a simple working solution and then optimize, or discuss the approach first?
```

```text
Can I take a minute to think before answering?
```

## Common interview friction points

| Friction point | Possible support |
|---|---|
| Vague interview format | Ask for interview type, length, and topics in advance |
| Live coding pressure | Ask about language, platform, and whether docs are allowed |
| Auditory processing load | Ask for written instructions or prompt in chat |
| Processing speed mismatch | Ask for a moment to think before answering |
| Anxiety during observation | Practice narration scripts and recovery phrases |
| Memory load | Keep a short notes page with stories and questions |
| Ambiguous expectations | Ask what skills the interview is designed to assess |

## Accommodation request examples

You do not have to overexplain your medical history to ask for a reasonable support.

### Written instructions

```text
Hi [Name],

Ahead of the interview, I wanted to ask whether written instructions or prompts can be shared during the technical portion. I process information best when I can reference the prompt visually, and this would help me perform at my best.

Thank you,
[Your Name]
```

### Extra processing time

```text
Hi [Name],

For the upcoming interview, I wanted to ask whether it would be possible to allow brief pauses for processing before answering technical questions. I do my best work when I can take a moment to organize my thoughts before responding.

Thank you,
[Your Name]
```

### Clarifying the format

```text
Hi [Name],

Could you share a little more detail about the interview format so I can prepare appropriately? For example, should I expect live coding, system design, a project discussion, behavioral questions, or another format?

Thank you,
[Your Name]
```

### Alternative to live coding, when appropriate

```text
Hi [Name],

I wanted to ask whether the process allows any alternatives to live coding, such as a take-home exercise or technical discussion. I understand the team needs to evaluate technical ability, and I am happy to complete an assessment that demonstrates my skills clearly.

Thank you,
[Your Name]
```

## During the interview: scripts that help

### When you need time

```text
I am going to take a moment to think through the structure before I answer.
```

### When the prompt is unclear

```text
I want to make sure I am solving the right problem. Can I clarify one part of the prompt?
```

### When you lose your train of thought

```text
Let me reset and summarize where I am.
```

### When you know the concept but not the exact term

```text
I may not be using the exact terminology, but the concept I am thinking of is...
```

### When you need to use notes

```text
I am going to reference my notes briefly so I can answer accurately.
```

## How to prepare if live coding is hard for you

Practice the interview behavior, not just the problems.

Try this:

1. Pick one easy problem.
2. Read the prompt out loud.
3. Restate it in your own words.
4. Ask 1-2 clarifying questions.
5. Explain the brute-force approach.
6. Explain one possible optimization.
7. Code slowly and narrate what you are doing.
8. Test one normal case and one edge case.
9. Summarize the time and space complexity.

The goal is not speed first. The goal is making your thinking visible.

## Personal notes page

Keep this short and easy to scan.

```text
My strongest project:

Hard bug/debugging story:

Conflict/collaboration story:

Failure/learning story:

Questions for team:

Reminder phrases:
- Let me think for a moment.
- Can I restate the prompt?
- The tradeoff is...
- I would start simple, then optimize.
```

## What candidates should not have to guess

A well-run process should be able to clarify:

- Interview format
- Interview length
- Skills being assessed
- Whether coding is live or take-home
- Whether documentation is allowed
- Whether a specific language is required
- Who to contact if something goes wrong

If the process cannot answer basic format questions, that may be a process maturity signal.

## For recruiters and hiring teams

Accessible prep does not mean lowering the bar.

It means reducing noise so the interview measures the right things:

- Technical skill
- Problem solving
- Judgment
- Communication
- Collaboration
- Role alignment

Better structure helps everyone, especially candidates who are capable but not optimized for vague, high-pressure performance rituals.

## Final reminder

You do not need to disclose everything about yourself to ask for what helps you perform.

Clarity, written context, processing time, and humane interview design are not special treatment. They are better signal design.

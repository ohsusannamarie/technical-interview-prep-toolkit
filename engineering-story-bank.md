# Engineering Story Bank

Technical candidates often have stronger experience than their interview answers show.

This template helps you turn projects, bugs, launches, migrations, incidents, research, and technical decisions into interview-ready stories.

## Why this matters

Interviewers are not just listening for tools and technologies. They are listening for:

- Ownership
- Judgment
- Problem-solving
- Tradeoffs
- Collaboration
- Impact
- Reflection

A strong technical story helps them understand how you think and what you actually owned.

## Story inventory

Use this list to find stories before you start polishing them.

| Story type | Example prompt |
|---|---|
| Technical depth | Tell me about a technically difficult project. |
| Debugging | Tell me about a hard bug or production issue. |
| Architecture | Tell me about a design decision you made. |
| Ambiguity | Tell me about a time requirements were unclear. |
| Collaboration | Tell me about working across teams. |
| Conflict | Tell me about a disagreement with a teammate or stakeholder. |
| Failure | Tell me about a mistake and what you learned. |
| Impact | Tell me about a project that improved something measurable. |
| Leadership | Tell me about influencing without authority. |
| Learning | Tell me about learning something quickly. |

## Project story template

```text
Project:

What was the problem?

Why did it matter?

Who used or cared about the outcome?

What was your specific role?

What were the constraints?

What technical decisions did you make?

What tradeoffs did you consider?

What was difficult?

How did you collaborate with others?

What was the result?

What did you learn?

What would you do differently now?
```

## 60-second version

Use this when an interviewer asks, “Tell me about a project you worked on.”

```text
I worked on [project/system] where the goal was [problem/outcome].

My role was [specific ownership].

The main challenge was [technical or business constraint].

I decided to [technical decision/action], because [reason/tradeoff].

The result was [impact/result/learning].
```

## Deep-dive version

Use this for technical follow-up questions.

```text
The context was...

The system/process originally worked like...

The problem was...

The options we considered were...

I chose/recommended...

The tradeoff was...

The hardest part was...

The impact was...

Looking back, I would...
```

## Technical decision template

Use this when discussing architecture, system design, tooling, frameworks, models, or implementation choices.

```text
Decision:

Options considered:

Constraints:

Why I chose this option:

Tradeoffs:

Risks:

How I validated it:

Outcome:
```

## Debugging story template

```text
What was broken?

How did you know something was wrong?

What signals, logs, metrics, tests, or user reports did you use?

What hypotheses did you test?

What was the root cause?

What fixed it?

What did you do to prevent it from happening again?
```

## Senior/staff-level story lens

For senior, staff, principal, or leadership roles, add:

- Who else was affected?
- What was the organizational impact?
- Did you align multiple teams?
- Did you change a pattern, system, or standard?
- Did you reduce future risk?
- Did you mentor others or improve decision quality?
- Did your work create leverage beyond your own output?

## Common weak answer patterns

| Weak answer | Stronger version |
|---|---|
| “I worked on the API.” | “I redesigned the API response flow to reduce latency and make downstream integration easier.” |
| “We migrated the system.” | “I owned the migration plan for X service, including dependency mapping, rollout sequencing, and rollback strategy.” |
| “I fixed bugs.” | “I investigated recurring production failures, identified the root cause, and added monitoring to catch the issue earlier.” |
| “I helped with the model.” | “I evaluated model performance, identified data quality issues, and changed the feature pipeline to improve reliability.” |

## Final check

Before the interview, make sure each story answers:

- What was the problem?
- What did you personally do?
- Why did your decision make sense?
- What changed because of your work?
- What did you learn?

If your answer only lists tasks, keep going. The interview gold is usually in the decisions, tradeoffs, and impact.

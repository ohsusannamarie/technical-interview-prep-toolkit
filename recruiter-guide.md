# Recruiter Guide: Sharing Technical Interview Prep

Candidates do better when they understand what they are preparing for.

This guide is for recruiters, sourcers, coordinators, and hiring teams who want to share prep resources in a way that is useful instead of overwhelming.

## The principle

Do not send a giant list of links with no context.

That creates more anxiety, not more readiness.

Useful prep answers four questions:

1. What kind of interview is this?
2. What skills will be evaluated?
3. What should the candidate focus on first?
4. What does good communication look like in this process?

## Recruiter prep flow

| Step | Recruiter action | Candidate impact |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Confirm the interview format | Reduces guessing |
| 2 | Identify assessed skills | Helps candidate prioritize |
| 3 | Share only relevant resources | Prevents link overload |
| 4 | Set expectations for communication | Helps candidates show their thinking |
| 5 | Invite clarifying questions | Builds trust and improves candidate experience |

## Before sending prep materials

Clarify these details internally:

- Interview format
- Interview length
- Interviewers or interviewer roles
- Skills being assessed
- Whether coding is live or take-home
- Whether the candidate can choose a language
- Whether documentation is allowed
- Whether the interview includes system design
- Whether the interview includes behavioral questions
- Expected seniority level
- Any company-specific preparation guidance

If you cannot answer these questions, that is useful signal. The process may need more structure.

## What to send candidates

A strong prep note usually includes:

- Interview format
- Topics to expect
- What not to over-focus on
- How to prepare efficiently
- Links to only the most relevant resources
- Encouragement to ask clarifying questions

## Sample prep message

```text
Hi [Name],

Ahead of your upcoming interview, I wanted to share a little context so you know what to expect.

This conversation will focus on [format / topic]. You can expect discussion around [skills or themes]. The team will be listening for how you approach problems, communicate tradeoffs, and explain your thinking - not just whether you jump straight to a perfect answer.

A few helpful ways to prepare:
- Review the job description and the parts of your background that connect most directly
- Practice explaining one or two relevant projects in detail
- Be ready to talk through your assumptions, constraints, and tradeoffs
- Prepare a few thoughtful questions for the team

Here are a few prep resources that may help:
- [Resource 1]
- [Resource 2]
- [Resource 3]

Please reach out if anything about the format is unclear. We want you to feel prepared, not surprised.

Best,
[Recruiter]
```

## Shorter candidate prep note

Use this when you need something quick but still useful.

```text
Hi [Name],

For your upcoming interview, expect a conversation focused on [topic / format]. The team will be looking for [skills / signals], especially how you explain your thinking and work through tradeoffs.

Best prep:
- Review the role description
- Prepare 1-2 relevant project examples
- Practice talking through your approach out loud
- Bring a few questions for the team

Helpful resources:
- [Resource]
- [Resource]

Let me know if anything feels unclear.
```

## What to avoid

Avoid saying:

- "Just be yourself"
- "It is pretty standard"
- "They may ask some technical questions"
- "Here are 47 links"
- "I am not sure what they will cover"

These may be well-intended, but they are not useful.

## Better alternatives

| Instead of | Say |
|---|---|
| "It is a technical interview" | "This will be a live coding interview focused on problem-solving and communication." |
| "Review the basics" | "Review arrays, hash maps, Big O, and edge cases." |
| "Be prepared for system design" | "Be ready to discuss requirements, APIs, data modeling, scale, reliability, and tradeoffs." |
| "They want someone senior" | "They will likely look for technical judgment, ownership, architecture thinking, and cross-functional communication." |
| "Just walk through your resume" | "Prepare 2-3 projects where you can explain the problem, your role, technical decisions, tradeoffs, and impact." |

## Prep by interview type

### Recruiter screen

Share guidance around:

- Role fit
- Motivation
- Compensation expectations
- Location or remote expectations
- Work authorization
- Timeline
- Candidate priorities

### Hiring manager interview

Share guidance around:

- Team context
- Project ownership
- Collaboration style
- Role expectations
- Scope and seniority
- Past project examples

### Coding interview

Share guidance around:

- Platform or environment
- Language expectations
- Problem-solving approach
- Communication
- Edge cases
- Time management

### System design interview

Share guidance around:

- Requirements gathering
- Architecture choices
- Data model
- APIs
- Scaling
- Reliability
- Tradeoffs
- Communication style

### Behavioral interview

Share guidance around:

- STAR or CAR storytelling
- Conflict
- Failure
- Collaboration
- Leadership
- Ambiguity
- Learning agility
- Ownership

## Prep guidance by seniority

### Early career

Candidates may need clarity on:

- Interview format
- Basic expectations
- Whether they can ask questions
- How much experience depth is expected
- How to discuss school, internship, bootcamp, or project work

### Mid-level

Candidates may need clarity on:

- Scope expectations
- Ownership examples
- Role-specific technical depth
- Collaboration with product, design, QA, or other teams

### Senior

Candidates may need clarity on:

- Architecture expectations
- Influence beyond assigned tasks
- Mentorship expectations
- Ambiguity and tradeoff discussion
- Cross-functional leadership

### Staff+ / Principal

Candidates may need clarity on:

- Organization-level impact
- Technical strategy
- Influence without direct authority
- Systems thinking
- Examples of simplifying complexity

## Candidate enablement checklist

Before the interview, the candidate should know:

- [ ] What type of interview this is
- [ ] How long it will last
- [ ] Who they are meeting with
- [ ] What skills are being assessed
- [ ] Whether they need to code, design, present, or discuss past work
- [ ] Which resources are actually relevant
- [ ] Who to contact with questions

## Why this matters

Candidate prep is not about giving away the answer key.

It is about reducing noise so the interview can measure the right things:

- Skill
- Judgment
- Communication
- Problem solving
- Role alignment

When candidates are left guessing, interviews often measure confidence, insider knowledge, or anxiety tolerance instead.

That is not a talent strategy. That is a vibes-based obstacle course.
